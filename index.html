Learning Graph Dynamics with Interaction Effects Propagation for Deformable Linear Objects Shape Control

Abstract

The robotic manipulation of deformable linear objects (DLOs) has broad applications, e.g., manufacturing and medical surgery. To complete such tasks, an accurate dynamics model for predicting the deformation is critical. However, due to the infinite dimensionality of the state space of DLOs and the complexity of their dynamics, it is hard to obtain accurate dynamics models theoretically.

Interaction Network (IN), which is based on Graph Neural Network (GNN), have succeeded in learning to model the dynamics of DLOs. However, it only considers local information in the graph at every time step and cannot handle instantaneous propagation of effects. In this paper, we adopt the IN dynamics model for capturing the interaction between neighboring segments in a DLO and use an attention mechanism to propagate interaction effects along the DLO. The learned dynamics model can then be coupled with Model Predictive Control (MPC) algorithms to enable a robot to plan behaviors for shape control of the DLO.

Evaluation of our dynamics model with synthetic and newly collected real-world data shows better accuracy in short-term and long-term predictions than existing methods both in the simulation and the real world. Experiments also demonstrate that the MPC framework with the proposed dynamics model can handle various challenging shaping tasks both in the simulation and the real world.

CONTROL USING LEARNED DYNAMICS

Trajectory Optimization under MPC Framework

The learned dynamics model is naturally differentiable. Given the model and a desired goal, we can perform forward simulation, optimizing the control inputs by minimizing a loss between simulated results and the goal.

The MPC is used to stabilize the trajectory by doing forward simulation at every time step as a way to compensate the simulation error

Real-world Experiments for Shape Control

A Kinova Gen3 robot manipulates one end of a DLO to different desired shapes, and the other end is fixed. In real-world experiments, the DLO is a 0.5-m USB cable and characterized by 8 features (red markers). An Intel Realsense D435 RGBD camera is utilized to obtain the positions of features of the DLO at 15Hz and 1280 x 720 resolution.
